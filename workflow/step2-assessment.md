**Step 2: Assessment**

**Purpose**

Identify security control gaps, evaluate risk, and establish clear understandings of the client's current security posture.
This step is the core of the entire audit, it is where the collected information from step 1 is translated into the actual risk facing the business.
Additionally, it is meant to turn the findings into a draft of the final deliverable assessment document presented to the client and communicate properaly to the relevant stakeholders (technical and nontechnical)
It is crucial for the final deliverable findings to be understandable to the cient and produce actionable insights into the analysis.


**Approach**

Assessment is based on the NIST CSF and a customized template tailored to communicate the assessment findings to the client.
The assessment template creates a presentable analysis and organizes it based on the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, it does this based on the core 5 functions and the new revisioned category governance.
  - Governance: establish and montior the org's cybersecurity management strategy, expectations, and policy
  - Identify: determine the current risk to the business
  - Protect: support the ability to use safeguards (controls) to prevent and reduce risks
  - Detect: provide systems to discover and analyze prossible cyber attacks and compromises 
  - Respond: create processes and abilities to take action regarding cyber incidents
  - Recover: involves procedures to restore assets and operations that were impacted by an incident

These categories allow for a high-level and structured system for evaluating the security posture, controls, and risks of an organization of any size or industry.

**Assessment Methodology**

The assessment follows a simple, repeatable process:
1. Identify assets, systems, and data
2. Identify threats and vulnerabilities 
3. Map threats to relevant vulnerabilities 
4. Evaluate likelihood and impact 
5. Assign risk levels

Scoring Model:

    • Likelihood: Low / Medium / High 
    • Impact: Low / Medium / High 
    • Risk Level: Derived from combined rating 

Note: The below categories does not exhaust the overall extent to which the analysis will audit the client's overall security risk.

---

**Key Assessment Categories**

**1. Asset and Data Identification**

Establish areas that need to be secured.
  - Systems, devices, and applications
  - Ownership and criticality
  - Data type and sensitivity
---
**2. Threat and Vulnerability Analysis**

Identify what poses a threat to an identified vulnerability.
- Threat sources:
	- External (attackers, phishing, malware)
	- Internal (human error, misuse)
 	- Environmental (device loss, disasters)
- Vulnerabilities:
	- Weak access controls
	- Lack of training
	- Unpatched systems
	- Single points of failure
Threat–vulnerability pairing is used to determine realistic risk scenarios

---

**3. Risk Analysis**
Each identified scenario is evaluated using:
- Likelihood -- probability of occurance
- Impact -- business consequences if realized
This produces a clear risk level used for prioritization.

---

**4. Current Security Posture**
Security controls are reviewed across the CSF categories:
  - Governance
  - Identify
  - Protect
  - Detect
  - Respond
  - Recover

The goal is to identify:
  - Existing Controls
  - Control gaps
  - Areas of weakness
___

**5. Risk Treatment**

For each identified risk:
- Mitigate (implement controls)
- Accept (acknowledge risk)
- Defer (address later)

Note: Only high-level recommendations are provided at this stage. Detailed implementation is handled separately through projects like the IRS WISP development.
___

**Output**

This step produces:
  - Documented findings
  - Risk ratings
  - Identified control gaps
  - High-level remediation guidance

**Key Principle**

The assessment step focuses on identifying and prioritizing real business risk and categorizing them in the risk analysis and identifying high-level treatment solutions.
